# Understanding Stocks: A Beginner's Guide

## What Is a Stock?

A stock (also called a share or equity) represents a small piece of ownership in a company. When you buy a stock, you become a partial owner — or shareholder — of that company.

Companies sell stocks to raise money for growth, research, or paying off debt. In return, shareholders may benefit from the company's success through rising stock prices or dividend payments.

## How Do Stocks Make Money?

There are two primary ways to make money from stocks:

### 1. Capital Appreciation
If you buy a stock at $50 and its price rises to $75, the $25 increase is called capital appreciation. You realize this gain when you sell the stock.

### 2. Dividends
Some companies share their profits with shareholders through regular payments called dividends. Not all stocks pay dividends — growth companies often reinvest profits instead.

## Key Stock Market Terms

- **Bull Market**: A period when stock prices are generally rising (typically 20%+ from recent lows)
- **Bear Market**: A period when stock prices are falling (typically 20%+ from recent highs)
- **Market Capitalization**: The total value of a company's outstanding shares (share price × number of shares)
- **P/E Ratio**: Price-to-Earnings ratio — compares a stock's price to its earnings per share
- **Volume**: The number of shares traded in a given time period

## Types of Stocks

### By Company Size
- **Large-cap**: Companies worth $10 billion+ (e.g., Apple, Microsoft)
- **Mid-cap**: Companies worth $2-10 billion
- **Small-cap**: Companies worth under $2 billion

### By Style
- **Growth stocks**: Companies expected to grow faster than average (often don't pay dividends)
- **Value stocks**: Companies trading below their perceived intrinsic value
- **Dividend stocks**: Companies known for consistent dividend payments

## Risks of Stock Investing

- **Market risk**: Overall market downturns can affect all stocks
- **Company risk**: Individual companies can underperform or go bankrupt
- **Volatility**: Stock prices can fluctuate significantly in short periods
- **Liquidity risk**: Some stocks may be difficult to sell quickly at fair prices

## Getting Started

1. **Start with index funds or ETFs** before picking individual stocks
2. **Only invest money you won't need** for at least 5 years
3. **Diversify** across different sectors and company sizes
4. **Invest regularly** through dollar-cost averaging
5. **Stay informed** but avoid making emotional decisions based on short-term news
